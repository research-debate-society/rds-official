Bioterrorism: A Global Challenge with Local Consequences in South Asia and Pakistan

By Bilal Rehman

Bioterrorism, defined as the intentional release of biological agents—such as bacteria, viruses, or toxins—to harm or intimidate civilian populations, poses a significant threat to global security. Understanding the implications of bioterrorism requires an examination of its political motivations, societal impacts, prevention strategies, ethical considerations, and historical lessons, particularly in the context of the world, South Asia, and Pakistan.

Global Context of Bioterrorism

Bioterrorism has become a pressing global issue due to advancements in biotechnology and the increasing accessibility of harmful biological agents. Historical events like the anthrax attacks in the United States following September 11, 2001, have highlighted vulnerabilities in public health systems and raised concerns about the potential for bioweapons to be used against civilian populations. The Biological Weapons Convention (1972) and the Geneva Protocol (1925) aim to prohibit the development and use of biological weapons; however, compliance remains a challenge as rogue states and terrorist organizations may not adhere to international agreements.

The societal consequences of bioterrorism can be profound. Economic disruption is a primary concern; bioterrorist attacks can lead to significant healthcare costs, loss of productivity, and damage to industries such as agriculture and tourism. For instance, during the COVID-19 pandemic, fears of bioterrorism were exacerbated by misinformation and public panic, illustrating how biological threats can lead to significant social unrest and erosion of trust in institutions.

South Asia's Vulnerability

In South Asia, political motivations for bioterrorism are influenced by regional conflicts and instability. Countries like India and Pakistan have complex geopolitical relationships that could potentially lead to the use of biological weapons as a means of warfare or terrorism. The presence of non-state actors in this region complicates security dynamics; groups may resort to bioterrorism to achieve their objectives or retaliate against perceived injustices.

The societal impacts of bioterrorism in South Asia could be severe due to existing vulnerabilities such as inadequate healthcare systems and high population density. The region has historically faced challenges related to infectious diseases; thus, a bioterrorist attack could exacerbate existing health crises. Moreover, economic repercussions would likely follow any attack, affecting livelihoods and leading to increased poverty levels.

Efforts to combat bioterrorism in South Asia must focus on enhancing regional cooperation among nations. International organizations can play a crucial role in facilitating dialogue and sharing best practices for biodefense strategies. Strengthening public health infrastructure is essential for improving preparedness against potential biological threats.

Pakistan's Specific Challenges

Pakistan faces unique challenges regarding bioterrorism due to its geopolitical position and internal security issues. The country has been a target for various terrorist organizations that may consider bioweapons as viable tools for achieving their goals. Additionally, tensions with neighbouring India could lead to escalated threats involving biological agents.

The potential societal impacts of bioterrorism in Pakistan are alarming. With a population exceeding 240 million and significant portions living in poverty, an outbreak caused by a bioterrorist attack could overwhelm an already strained healthcare system. Furthermore, public panic resulting from such an attack could lead to social unrest and exacerbate existing political tensions.

To address these challenges effectively, Pakistan must prioritize developing a comprehensive biodefense strategy that includes:

  • Strengthening Public Health Infrastructure: Investing in healthcare facilities and training healthcare workers to respond effectively to biological threats.
  • Enhancing Surveillance Systems: Implementing robust surveillance systems to detect outbreaks early.
  • International Collaboration: Engaging with international organizations for knowledge sharing and resource allocation.

Ethical Considerations

Ethical dilemmas surrounding bioweapons research must also be considered within Pakistan's context. The potential misuse of scientific advancements poses risks not only locally but also globally. Ensuring that research adheres to ethical standards is crucial for preventing bioweapons proliferation.

Conclusion

Bioterrorism represents a multifaceted threat that transcends borders, affecting global security dynamics while presenting unique challenges in regions like South Asia and specifically Pakistan. Understanding the political motivations behind such acts, their societal impacts, and implementing effective prevention strategies are critical steps toward safeguarding public health and ensuring national security. By fostering resilience through education, infrastructure investment, and international cooperation, countries can better prepare for potential biological threats while addressing ethical considerations inherent in this complex issue.

By Abdul Rehman

Fourteen years have passed since the last Labour Government, led by former Prime Minister Gordon Brown, from 2007 to 2010. Sir Keir Starmer, the Leader of the Labour Party, has achieved a landslide victory over the Conservatives and has been elected as the new Prime Minister of the United Kingdom. This election marks the Conservative Party's worst electoral defeat in its history. The First Labour Government, formed in 1924, ended abruptly in November of the same year, and this event remains a significant part of Labour Party mythology, continuing to haunt British politics even today.

The Great War and The Decline of The Aristocratic Class

The Great War, known as the war to end all wars, was characterised by brutal trench warfare and the use of poisonous gas. The introduction of modern military vehicles such as tanks and aeroplanes made the war extremely costly in lives lost. While the exact number of casualties remains uncertain, most sources report that over 8 million soldiers died in WW1. However, the two outbreaks of Spanish Flu in 1918 further complicated the records. Trench warfare, a defining feature of the conflict, involved soldiers from opposing sides digging in to protect themselves. It was brutal; soldiers often gained only a few metres of ground for great losses. Life in the trenches was exceedingly stressful due to the loud sounds of artillery targeting their positions, as well as poor hygiene and the spread of disease by rats and lice.

The war resulted in the loss of many members of the British ruling class, leaving many families without heirs to carry on their legacy. It had a profound effect on the socio-economic structure of British society. The transfer of power to the general public significantly impacted the ruling class, as individuals from lower social strata rose to positions of authority. This shift in the British class structure made the aristocracy more vulnerable to new forms of accountability. As entire lineages vanished, estates passed out of aristocratic hands — creating a power vacuum that returning veterans and their trade-union allies were primed to fill.

Communism Gains Traction

The trenches left veterans hungry not just for reform, but for deeper change. Karl Marx's century-old critique of industrial capitalism now resonated with ex-servicemen. Marxist communist ideologies gained traction following the Bolshevik revolution in 1917, which led to the abdication of the Czars. Capitalising on anti-war sentiment, Vladimir Lenin successfully overthrew the Provisional Government and ultimately withdrew Russia from the war. The global spread of Communist ideologies during the post-Great War period was facilitated by the favourable environment that followed the war.

The creation of the Third International, known as COMINTERN, by Vladimir Lenin played a key role in supporting the worldwide communist revolution. This international organisation promoted the spread of communism and backed communist parties across various nations, effectively representing the interests of the USSR. The formation of the Communist Party of Great Britain (CPGB) was inspired by the revolution in Eastern Europe: the CPGB aimed to emulate the Bolshevik Revolution and overthrow the capitalist system in Britain. Like many other communist parties in Europe and beyond, the CPGB was closely linked with COMINTERN and was heavily influenced by it.

Labour's Rise and The Zinoviev Letter

The Labour Party's gradual rise, its alliance with the Trade Union Congress, and its overall pledge to improve the lives of the working class and ensure their representation resonated with individuals who felt neglected by their existing representation. The decline of the Liberal Party at that period boosted Labour, and Arthur Henderson's leadership demonstrated Labour's capacity to act independently. The emergence of the Labour Party as a key force in British politics made it a formidable opponent to the Conservatives, eventually resulting in a government formed with the support of Asquith and his Liberals.

During this time, Ramsay MacDonald's government introduced various laws and policies aimed at improving housing, education, and tackling unemployment. However, lacking a clear majority, they struggled to pass socialist legislation aligned with their agenda. They were in office from January to November 1924, which limited their ability to pass new laws. During that time the Labour government officially recognised the Soviets in February 1924 through a series of concessions exchanged for ambassadors. This decision faced strong opposition from the Conservatives, who opposed engaging with the Soviets and instead supported a policy of isolation.

Just before the 1924 elections, the Daily Mail published a letter purportedly written by Grigory Zinoviev, the head of COMINTERN, urging the CPGB to carry out seditious activities to ignite a Bolshevik proletarian revolution in Britain. Although Zinoviev denied the letter's authenticity, it influenced public perception of the Labour Party during the 1924 election. Consequently, the Labour Party was perceived as sympathetic to the USSR and communist ideologies. Supporters of Labour claimed that their defeat in the 1924 general elections was influenced by the Zinoviev Letter.

Legacy and Lessons

Following the formation of Stanley Baldwin's government, a committee was established to examine the letter, ultimately confirming its authenticity. However, no further investigations followed, and historians widely agree that it was a forgery. It is important to note that the Labour Party's loss cannot be solely attributed to the Zinoviev Letter; other factors, including their policies and the political climate at the time, also played a role.

Elections happen around the world, prompting us to reflect on past electoral disputes where even minor errors could influence the outcome. This highlights the importance of transparent communication between political parties. Active participation in democratic processes is crucial to counter misinformation and propaganda, which can influence public opinion. This is especially vital given the rising cyber threats and false information in our interconnected global community, where individuals can be misled by fabricated stories designed to serve personal interests.

By Dr Ali Salman — March 23, 2026 · 3 min read

Editorial Note

There is a common assumption in development economics that strong institutions must come before unleashing the market. Dr. Ali Salman challenges this directly: if we wait for Pakistan to have perfectly functioning, textbook institutions, we might just be waiting forever. Drawing on research from the Chinese experience, he argues that markets and institutions can co-evolve rather than follow a strict sequence, and that Pakistan's continuing expansion of the state into the economy has been a rigid obstacle to sustained growth. We are featuring this article as part of our ongoing series on Pakistan's political economy, and as a follow-up to our discussion on the budget.

A common critique of market economics, especially in the developing world, is that it needs perfect institutions to work. These market imperfections, such as monopolies, dominance of firms, state capture by interest groups, weak rule of law, and information asymmetry, pose structural problems for markets to function, critics argue.

It is believed that we ought to remove these market imperfections or inefficiencies first to let a market economy emerge and flourish. In short, administrative and legal institutions should pre-empt a spontaneous, market-led order; otherwise, we will face the risk of exploitation and sub-optimal socio-economic outcomes.

This is the essence of contemporary work on institutional economics, which has formed the basis of the well-known book "Why Nations Fail: The Origins of Power, Prosperity and Poverty" by Acemoglu and Robinson, who received the Nobel Prize in Economics in 2024.

There is another view, however. In this article, I argue, using the framework of co-evolution developed by Yuen Yuen Ang in her book "How China Escaped Poverty", that markets do not have to wait for strong institutions. A neat temporal separation between two events — establishment of institutions and sustained economic growth — is impossible. Both co-evolve.

In the case of China, as Yuen masterfully demonstrates in her book, economic development actually preceded the emergence of formal, transparent institutions — her insight: "build markets with weak institutions".

China, she tells us, did not start neatly with a set of well-developed institutions. It began with a messy and chaotic reorganisation of its economy. Once it saw the fruits of economic development, the government and society started building institutional competence.

As China progressed, it also invested in building formal institutions, which complemented the speed of economic development. Thus, we can see a feedback loop — economic development, institutional strengthening, more economic development, and so on. Yuen argues that the role of institutions in advanced industrial economies is to preserve markets.

At the time of independence, Pakistan had "weak institutions", but there was a strong entrepreneurial and merchant class that migrated from India and settled in Karachi. This network of Muslim business elite played a critical role in early economic development. The state played catch-up. It established development finance institutions and a supportive network.

However, it went to another extreme. Instead of nurturing entrepreneurial talent, it started distributing capital, quotas, and preferences. It took the route of state capitalism. The nationalisation of the 1970s was, in one way, a consequence — and not a reaction — of state capitalism! The first sign of commanding heights was the forceful takeover of Orient Airways by Pakistan's parliament in 1955 and the incorporation of Pakistan Airlines.

While privatisation and deregulation started in the 1990s, and did bring a wave of economic reforms, we could not completely uproot the statist foundations of our economy.

The most telling example of this contest — between the state and the market — is the unbundling of Wapda. All electricity distribution companies were corporatised, and a new entity, Pakistan Electric Power Company (Pepco), was established in 1998 for "effective monitoring and oversight of the distribution companies (DISCOs)".

Thus, the state kept its footprint, which has only increased. A quarter century later, we are now witnessing the revival of Pepco as Power Planning and Monitoring Company (PPMC). The energy sector continues to drag our markets and has become an Achilles heel of the state.

Learning from the episode of PPMC, let me offer a new formulation of a well-known reality. When an entrepreneur sets up a company and it fails, they exit and close the company. When the state establishes a company and it fails, the state injects more money to revive it.

Historically, we have suppressed and curbed entrepreneurial talent in the country through two channels. One, by strangulation in the name of regulation. We are setting up more regulatory bodies with each passing day. At the federal level alone, there are 122 regulatory bodies. There are multiple bodies to keep a check on the same business, performing the same function.

Second, the continued and expanding footprint of the government in the market. The federal government has exited the airlines business, but the Punjab government has entered through Air Punjab. When a government enters a business, it distorts the level playing field. It has access to guaranteed capital and can arm-twist regulatory bodies. At entry level, no private firm can do it.

The government has enacted the Asaan Karobar Act and has undertaken a comprehensive regulatory guillotine process. These are good signs. It will be a huge challenge for the government to use these mechanisms to eradicate the statist roots of our economy and trim them where necessary.
